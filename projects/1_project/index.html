EMGW

Electro-Magnetic follow-up of Gravitational-Wave events in Radio

Gravitational waves, a new way to probe the universe, were first predicted by Albert Einstein in 1916. It took nearly a 100 years, for us to detect them for the first time and finally in 2015, a merger of two black holes was detected by the Light Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory (LIGO) detectors in Livingston, Louisiana and Hanford, Washington, USA. The 2017 Nobel Prize in Physics was awarded to Rainer Weiss, Barry C. Barish and Kip S. Thorne for contributions to building the LIGO detectors and for the detection of gravitational waves.

Simulation of the first ever LIGO detection of gravitational waves from merging black holes. Image from https://www.ligo.caltech.edu/image/ligo20160211d


Since this first discovery, three observing runs have been completed as of 27 March 2020 17:00 UTC, with many compact object mergers being discovered, most of which are binary black hole mergers.

Mergers detected by LIGO. Image from https://www.ligo.caltech.edu/image/ligo20200623a


GW170817, the first binary neutron star merger to be heard by LIGO and seen by various electromagnetic instruments came as a welcome surprise for the community and has put multi-messenger astronomy on the forefront. I had worked on trying to localise this source in the X-ray using the Cadmium Zinc Telluride Imager (CZTI) onboard the Indian multi-wavelength astronomy satellite, AstroSat.

Localising GW170817. The white circle is the earth as seen by CZTI. A non-detection due to the source being behind the earth helped to exclude the shaded region. Image from Kasliwal, M. M., Nakar, E., Singer, L. P., et al. 2017, Science, 358, 1559


I am currently working on late time follow-up of radio emission from GW170817 with the Jansky Very Large Array (VLA). As a part of the Jansky VLA mapping of Gravitational Wave bursts as Afterglows in Radio (JAGWAR) collaboration, formed to improve chances of discovering radio counterparts with the VLA, I have worked on radio follow-up some of the LIGO O3 candidates.
